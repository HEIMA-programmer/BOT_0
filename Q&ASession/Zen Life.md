### Short Answer (6)

1. **How did Mark first study Zen?**  
   **Answer:** He studied it in a religion class during college.  
   **Explanation:** He says he learned about it in college through a class on religion.

2. **How does Mark define Zen in simple terms?**  
   **Answer:** He says Zen is about appreciating small moments and being in the present moment.  
   **Explanation:** That is the clearest summary he gives in the conversation.

3. **What natural details does Mark mention as examples of Zen appreciation?**  
   **Answer:** The beauty in nature, the wind on your face, and the sound of birds and trees.  
   **Explanation:** He uses those sensory details to explain the feeling of Zen.

4. **What does Mark say about being “good” or “bad” at Zen?**  
   **Answer:** He says there is no good or bad in Zen.  
   **Explanation:** He rejects the idea that Zen should be judged in that way.

5. **Why does Mark think constant Zen awareness is unattainable?**  
   **Answer:** Because the mind naturally drifts to the future or returns to the past.  
   **Explanation:** He says people will inevitably think about tomorrow or remember old experiences.

6. **What does Mark do when he notices he is overthinking?**  
   **Answer:** He brings his focus back to what he is doing in the present moment.  
   **Explanation:** He describes this as his personal way of returning to Zen awareness.

   **Explanation:** He directly links Zen to mindfulness in the conversation.

   **Answer:** He says Zen can be found in noticing and appreciating simple sensory experiences in nature.  
   **Explanation:** His examples include wind, birds, trees, and the beauty around you.

   **Answer:** It shows she understands Zen as being fully present in what is happening right now.
